The New Pumps: A First Look

Early read on the variable-speed pool pump & new hot tub pumps (installed Apr 11, 2026) vs. a year of old-pump data — Privette Household, Virginia Beach VA

TL;DR — Promising, but it's early

The new pumps went in April 11, 2026, so there are only about 10 weeks of post-install data — and almost no deep-summer days yet, which is exactly when a pool pump matters most. With that caveat front and center:

On equally warm days (~80°F, when the pool is actually running), the house now uses about 17% less — roughly 95 → 79 kWh/day. The reduction scales with temperature: big in warm weather, near zero in cool shoulder weeks when the pool barely runs and there's little pump load to save. The single cleanest signal is a 20% lower overnight baseline — the variable-speed and hot tub pumps sipping power instead of gulping it. Worth roughly $2.50–3/day on hot days. Treat this as a directional first look; the real test is summer 2026.

The Key Insight: Savings Scale With Temperature

Rather than one blunt average, this groups every day by its temperature and compares old-pump vs new-pump days within each band. That controls for weather and reveals the real pattern: the hotter it gets — the more the pool runs — the more the new pump saves.

Avg Daily kWh by Temperature Band — Old Pump vs. New Pumps

In cool bands (50–70°F) the gap is small — the pool runs little, so an efficient pump saves little. In warm bands (75–85°F) the new-pump bars drop 14–19% below old. That's the variable-speed pump earning its keep when the pool is working hardest. Note the new-pump warm bars rest on only a handful of days so far.

The Load-Curve Fingerprint

Averaging every half-hour across matched warm days (~80°F) shows the daily “shape.” The old single-speed pump created a tall midday block (a big motor on a daytime timer). The variable-speed pump runs low and continuous instead — so the block flattens and the remaining peak is just afternoon air conditioning.

Average Half-Hourly Load — Old vs. New Pumps (both ~80°F)

What changed in the shape: the old curve peaked at midday (the pump); the new curve's peak is lower and shifts to ~3 PM (air conditioning). Biggest measurable drop is the overnight floor, −20% (1.36 → 1.09 kWh/half-hr) — consistent with the more efficient pumps humming quietly through the night rather than the old hot-tub pumps' heavier draw.

What It's Worth, and the Rate Headwind

Recent bills imply an all-in rate around 16.7¢/kWh (Apr–Jun 2026: $320–331/mo). At that rate, the ~16 kWh/day warm-weather saving is worth about $2.50–3/day when it's hot — less in mild months. Don't annualize the hot-day figure; the pool runs far less in winter.

Running to stand still: Dominion's first base-rate increase since 1992 took effect January 1, 2026 (~+$11.24/mo for a typical bill), with another ~$2.36/mo in January 2027. The pump savings partly just offset rising rates — which is the whole reason to keep squeezing usage.

How I Did This (Methodology)

Data: Dominion interval export for account 6552484617 (half-hourly kWh, Jun 22 2025 → Jun 21 2026, 365 days); Dominion payment export (recent bills); Virginia Beach daily mean temperature from Open-Meteo to control for weather; public Dominion/SCC rate filings.

Regime split: Install date Apr 11 2026 (provided). Old = before that date (293 days), New = on/after (72 days).

Weather control: Because the new-pump window is spring/early-summer and the old window spans all seasons, days are compared within temperature bands and on a matched-temperature basis, so equipment — not weather — drives the difference. Pool usage is itself seasonal, so warm-band comparisons (summer-old vs early-summer-new, both pool-active) are the most apples-to-apples.

Big caveat: only ~10 weeks and ~18 genuinely warm days of new-pump data exist, topping out near 86°F. The deep-summer days where a pool pump saves the most aren't in the data yet. This is a directional first look, not a final verdict. Behavior changes (not just the pumps) also contribute.

For a definitive number: the clean comparison is the same calendar window year-over-year — Apr 11–Jun 21 2025 (old) vs 2026 (new). The 2025 side sits in the earlier three-year export, not this file; adding it would isolate the equipment with no seasonal guesswork.
